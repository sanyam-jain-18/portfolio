## The problem

The platform&apos;s ads delivery layer had grown organically over several years.
What started as a single delivery script had absorbed a decade of partner
integrations, tracking pixels, viewability vendors, consent-management hooks,
and per-surface customisations. The result was a monolithic module that
everyone was afraid to touch — every change risked a regression somewhere
unrelated, and the team had stopped landing structural improvements because
the blast radius was unknowable.

The brief was simple in shape and hard in detail: untangle this without
breaking the revenue surface that depends on it, while opening the door to
new ad formats and partner integrations that the current shape blocks.

## The approach

I led an architectural review across three lenses — delivery pipeline,
partner integration surface, and observability — and used what we found to
draft a target shape rather than a rewrite.

The target separated four concerns that had been fused together:

- **Configuration resolution** — turning a page context into a set of ad slot
  decisions. Pure, testable, no I/O.
- **Delivery orchestration** — sequencing requests, races, and timeouts
  across multiple supply paths.
- **Partner adapters** — one module per vendor, with a minimal contract
  surface and explicit lifecycle hooks.
- **Instrumentation** — a single event taxonomy that every other layer emits
  into, decoupled from the vendor-specific tracking surfaces.

This wasn&apos;t a rewrite. It was a sequence of behaviour-preserving
refactors, each independently shippable. The team could reach for any
checkpoint and still have a green build, a working ad surface, and a
revenue-safe deploy.

## What this unlocked

- New partner integrations went from multi-week, multi-team efforts to a
  single adapter module behind a well-defined contract.
- The configuration resolver became unit-testable in isolation, which moved
  a class of regressions out of QA cycles and into pre-merge tests.
- The instrumentation taxonomy gave product and revenue teams a single
  source of truth for delivery-funnel reporting — replacing three
  disagreeing dashboards.
- The codebase finally had a shape that new engineers could be onboarded
  into without a week of pairing.

## What I&apos;d call out as lessons

> The refactor mattered less than the sequencing. A big-bang rewrite would
> have been politically defensible and operationally fatal.

A few things I keep coming back to from this work:

- **Architecture is a series of behaviour-preserving commits.** Every step
  should land independently. If a step can&apos;t, that&apos;s a signal the
  boundary you&apos;re drawing is wrong, not that the step needs to be
  bigger.
- **Contracts before code.** Every refactor in this project started by
  writing the interface between the new modules first, and then making both
  sides conform. The order matters.
- **Instrumentation is architecture.** A common event taxonomy is what
  let the team trust the new shape was actually behaving like the old one.
  Without that, every refactor would have been a coin flip.
