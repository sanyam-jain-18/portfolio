## The problem

A portfolio of consumer-facing web properties — news, lifestyle, commerce —
all sharing a common platform layer, all showing different shades of the
same Core Web Vitals problem. LCP was slow on key landing pages, CLS was
jumping on the surfaces that monetised, and INP regressions were sneaking
in unnoticed because the platform team didn&apos;t have a budget that
broke a build.

The brief was to bring measured user-experience metrics back inside an
acceptable band — without slowing down the product teams who were
landing features on top of the platform.

## The approach

I worked across the whole pipeline, but the largest wins came from three
threads:

- **Image delivery.** Audited the asset pipeline end-to-end. Replaced
  unbounded image dimensions with responsive `srcset` tuned per breakpoint;
  moved hero images to a dimensions-pinned format with explicit width and
  height to eliminate layout shift; routed the surface through a scaler
  service so above-the-fold images could be optimised at edge.
- **Render-blocking audit.** Walked through the critical path on the slowest
  templates with the team. Pulled third-party scripts off the critical
  path, deferred consent-management chains where the contract allowed it,
  and inlined the small slice of CSS that actually paints above the fold.
- **Hybrid app state-sync.** The mobile surface ran the web app inside a
  WebView with a native state singleton outside. State updates from the
  native side were being lost across reloads, and the SPA lifecycle on
  re-entry was firing effects against stale closures. Fixed by formalising
  a state-rehydration contract that handled both SPA-route and
  full-reload re-entries explicitly.

## What this unlocked

- Measurable, sustained LCP and CLS improvements across the surfaces that
  mattered most — landing pages, article reads, and the conversion
  funnels.
- A perf budget that broke a build before regressions reached production,
  rather than a dashboard nobody checked.
- A documented hybrid-app re-entry contract that other teams could ship
  against, instead of debugging the same state-staleness bug every quarter.
- A team habit of reading real-user-monitoring data before believing
  lab-test improvements.

## What I&apos;d call out as lessons

> Performance is a budgeting problem, not a one-shot optimisation problem.
> The work isn&apos;t over when LCP improves — it&apos;s over when LCP
> can&apos;t silently regress.

Things I keep returning to from this work:

- **RUM beats Lighthouse.** Lab metrics are a starting point. The number
  that matters is what your real users actually feel, segmented by device
  and connection.
- **Layout shift is mostly a discipline problem.** Pin dimensions on every
  image, every ad slot, every dynamically-injected component. The hard
  part isn&apos;t fixing it — it&apos;s preventing the next regression.
- **The state-sync contract is the bug.** Stale closures across same-render
  effects, state surviving WebView reloads, SPA-route effects firing
  against pre-route state — these all look like different bugs but they
  share one root cause: an implicit contract that nobody wrote down.
- **Performance budgets live in CI or they don&apos;t live at all.** A
  Slack channel of complaints about a regression is a budget that already
  failed.
